How Assembly Works (V3.5.1)

Mark Mirra authors the coaching methodology in Markdown via Braintrust Playground. At runtime, the wrapInXml() function wraps the Markdown prompt inside XML tags and injects all 81 coaching items (66 named moves + 8 frameworks + 6 quotes + 1 example) as structured <move> elements with governance fields (source, admission_date, rubric_score). V3.5.1 adds an ethics guardrail (~400 tokens) for corporate policy redirects. Anthropic's research shows XML-structured prompts improve Claude's performance by up to 40%. The model selects relevant moves based on the user's situation and weaves them into coaching naturally.

V3.3 → V3.4: What Changed and Why

Six significant changes in the prompt architecture, each driven by specific stakeholder feedback or research findings.

Fix + New 43 Named Aligned Moves (was 35, and NOT injected)
Before (V3.3) 35 moves existed in code but were never sent to the model. The ALIGNED_MOVES array was defined but the wrapInXml() function was not called on the prompt path. Moves were orphaned.
After (V3.4) 43 moves XML-wrapped and injected into every conversation. Both the local V3 path and the Braintrust path now call wrapInXml(). Tom's NSFD review added 8 new moves (Calibrated Question, Accusation Audit, "That's Right" Target, Black Swan Hunt, Leverage Triangle, Rule of Three, Gentle No, Reality Bend).
Why Tom Hilgeman's ASF + NSFD review (357 items: 217 approved, 140 rejected) identified 8 moves that deserved named status. Separately, a production audit revealed the move injection bug — the array existed since V3.2 but was never wired into the prompt assembly function. Both fixes shipped together.
New Priority Diagnostic — "What's the ONE term you must win?"
Before (V3.3) Ali waited until exchange 3 to trigger calibration questions. By then, coaching was already 2 turns generic because it didn't know what the user valued most.
After (V3.4) Ali asks "Which terms do you need to maximize here?" and "Where do you have flexibility?" within exchanges 1-2. This creates the strategic foundation for identifying asymmetric trades and protecting what matters.
Why Mark Mirra requested this Feb 12: "Ali needs to ask about terms to maximize earlier." Without knowing priorities, coaching defaults to generic advice. With priorities, Ali can identify trades where the user gives up something cheap for something expensive.
New Counterparty Perspective Rotation
Before (V3.3) Ali coached exclusively from the user's perspective. No modeling of what the counterparty was thinking, optimizing for, or constrained by.
After (V3.4) Before recommending tactics, Ali models the counterparty's likely position: "If I'm sitting on their side of the table, here's what I'm thinking..." Considers their optimizations, constraints, and likely responses to suggested approaches.
Why Research shows reasoning models naturally simulate perspectives when instructed. Explicit instruction amplifies this ability. The best negotiators anticipate the other side's moves before making their own. Ali now does this visibly, giving users a preview of likely counterparty reactions.
New Anti-Probability Guardrail
Before (V3.3) Ali generated responses like "There's a 60% chance they'll accept your opening offer" — false precision that undermined coaching credibility.
After (V3.4) Qualitative likelihood only: "likely," "possible but less common," "unlikely given what you've described." Coaches the user to prepare for multiple scenarios rather than anchoring on a predicted outcome.
Why Tom Hilgeman flagged this Jan 26: false precision undermines coaching credibility. A 60% prediction gives the user an anchor that may be completely wrong. Qualitative framing encourages preparation for multiple scenarios, which is the actual goal of coaching.
Improved Softened Instruction Markers
Before (V3.3) 5 uses of "CRITICAL:" and "NEVER:" markers caused Claude to over-trigger — producing verbose 4-6 point responses when 2-3 were appropriate. The model treated every instruction as equally urgent.
After (V3.4) WHY-clause explanations that achieve the same constraint naturally. Instead of "CRITICAL: Never ask more than 2 follow-ups," the prompt explains why — "more than 2 follow-ups turns coaching into a survey, and users disengage." The model internalizes the reasoning, not just the rule.
Why Anthropic's Claude 4.x guidance: aggressive markers like "CRITICAL" cause over-compliance. The model over-indexes on avoiding the prohibited behavior and compensates by being overly verbose elsewhere. WHY-clauses produce the same behavioral constraint with better output quality.
New 3 Real Example Exchanges
Before (V3.3) Zero examples in the prompt. The model inferred the desired register, tone, and response structure from instructions alone — inconsistent results.
After (V3.4) 3 exchanges from Mark Mirra's actual coaching sessions: (1) brake-rotors-seller with tiered pricing, (2) new-supplier opening with hunger test, (3) "run it up the food chain" counterparty pattern. Each demonstrates the target register: concise, insight-first, one thread at a time.
Why Anthropic research shows 3-5 examples improve prompt performance up to 40%. Examples are the most effective way to communicate register, tone, and response structure. The model learns from demonstrated behavior more effectively than from described behavior.

V3.4 → V3.5.1: What Changed and Why

Two releases: V3.5.0 (prompt architecture overhaul) and V3.5.1 (knowledge expansion + ethics guardrail).

V3.5.0 Stage 1 ADOPT — Identity, Coaching OS, Value Templates, Scope Boundaries
Before (V3.4) Identity and coaching instructions were spread across multiple prompt sections with overlapping guidance. Value template language was inconsistent. Scope boundaries existed but lacked structured enforcement.
After (V3.5.0) Four sections rewritten with ADOPT status: (1) Identity block consolidated into single authoritative section, (2) Coaching OS unified with consistent instruction patterns, (3) Value templates standardized across all response types, (4) Scope boundaries restructured with clear redirect patterns.
Why Mark Mirra approved all 4 ADOPT items on Mar 11. The identity rewrite eliminates conflicting instructions that caused inconsistent coaching voice. Coaching OS unification reduces prompt token count while improving behavioral consistency.
V3.5.0 Stage 2 MERGE — Deal Map + Red Flags Consolidation
Before (V3.4) Deal Map and Red Flags were separate prompt sections with redundant pattern-matching logic. Both referenced similar counterparty behaviors but with different terminology.
After (V3.5.0) Merged into unified sections that eliminate duplication while preserving all detection patterns. Terminology aligned to Aligned vocabulary. Two MERGE items approved by Mark.
Why Redundancy in the prompt wastes tokens and can cause conflicting guidance when the model encounters the same concept described two different ways. Merging creates a single source of truth for each behavioral pattern.
V3.5.1 HTWF Integration — 23 Moves, 8 Frameworks, 6 Quotes via Admission Rubric
Before (V3.5.0) 43 named moves from 3 sources (ASF 22, GTY 13, NSFD 8). HTWF source existed in the knowledge store (107 items) but no moves had been elevated to named status. No admission rubric for new move approval.
After (V3.5.1) 66 named moves from 4 sources (ASF 22, GTY 13, NSFD 8, HTWF 23). Each HTWF candidate scored on a 5-dimension rubric (actionability, coaching fit, source fidelity, distinctiveness, complementarity). Additionally: 8 coaching frameworks, 6 coaching quotes, 1 example — 81 total items XML-injected. All items now carry governance fields (source, admission_date, rubric_score).
Why Carnegie's interpersonal principles fill a gap in Ali's coaching: relationship-building, rapport, and influence tactics that complement the harder negotiation tactics from NSFD/GTY/ASF. The 5-dimension admission rubric ensures quality control as the move library scales — every move must earn its place.
V3.5.1 Ethics Guardrail — Corporate Policy Redirect (~400 tokens)
Before (V3.5.0) No explicit ethics guardrail in the prompt. Ali could be steered toward coaching tactics that conflict with corporate procurement policies, anti-bribery regulations, or HR guidelines without triggering a redirect.
After (V3.5.1) Dedicated <ethics_guardrail> XML section (~400 tokens) with pattern: (1) acknowledge the user's intent, (2) raise the corporate policy question, (3) offer an ethical alternative that achieves a similar outcome. Triggered when coaching requests conflict with standard corporate policies.
Why Enterprise customers require responsible AI guardrails. The pattern preserves Ali's coaching relationship (no lecturing) while ensuring the platform doesn't coach users into policy violations. The redirect offers a constructive alternative rather than a flat refusal.
V3.5.1 Governance Fields on All Moves
Before (V3.5.0) Moves were injected with name, description, and source only. No metadata about when they were approved, by whom, or their quality score.
After (V3.5.1) Every <move> element now includes: source (book abbreviation), admission_date (ISO date of approval), and rubric_score (5-dimension quality score). This creates an auditable trail for the coaching knowledge base.
Why As the move library grows from 43 to 66+ items, governance becomes critical. Stakeholders need to know when a move was added, what quality bar it met, and which source it came from. This also enables future features like deprecation tracking and A/B testing of move effectiveness.

Side-by-Side Comparisons

How different sources handle the same negotiation challenge — and how Ali synthesizes them.

GTY

Principled Approach

Separate the people from the problem. Avoid positional bargaining. Use objective criteria to break deadlocks. "Be hard on the problem, soft on the person." Focus on interests, not positions. Invent options for mutual gain. When they attack, don't attack back — reframe the attack as an attack on the problem.

NSFD

Tactical Empathy

Label their emotion: "It seems like you're frustrated with the timeline." Use mirrors to draw them out. Deploy an Accusation Audit before the meeting — name every negative thought they might have about you. Seek "that's right" instead of "yes." Use calibrated questions to make them solve your problem: "How am I supposed to do that?"

Ali's Synthesis

Start with tactical empathy to lower the temperature (NSFD: label, mirror, accusation audit). Once they feel heard, redirect to the problem with principled framing (GTY: interests, objective criteria). Use the HTWF principle of making them feel important while the ASF Playbook Read diagnoses which trained pattern they're running. The sequence matters: empathy first, then substance.

NSFD

Calibrated Questions

Open with "what" and "how" questions that give the counterparty the illusion of control. "What does a good outcome look like for you?" Never start with "why" — it triggers defensiveness. The first question should extract their constraints without revealing yours. Let them anchor first if you don't know the range.

3DN

Backward Mapping

Don't start at the table. Map the negotiation backward from the desired outcome. Who needs to say yes last? Who influences them? Approach the sequence in reverse — build momentum through preliminary agreements with easier parties before confronting the hardest. The opening move might be a meeting that happens weeks before the "real" negotiation.

Ali's Synthesis

Map the landscape first (3DN: who decides, who blocks, what order). Then open at the table with calibrated questions (NSFD: "what does success look like?"). Use the ASF Priority Diagnostic to surface which terms matter most. If the counterparty is new, lead with HTWF rapport-building. The opening strategy depends on whether this is a one-shot (start at the table) or multi-party (start away from it).

Before V3.4

Ali Leaked Context

In Mark Mirra's testing, Ali suggested draft messages that included confidential context the user had shared: canceled orders, price floors, internal deadlines. The user said "I have rotors from a canceled order" and Ali drafted outreach saying "We have rotors available from a canceled OEM order" — revealing distressed inventory to the counterparty.

After V3.4

Dynamic Confidentiality Guard

Two-layer defense: (1) Pre-generation guard extracts per-conversation sensitive items (price floors/ceilings, confidential context, walk-away points) and blocks them from draft messages. (2) Post-generation monitor scans outputs for leaked information. The prompt now includes explicit WRONG/RIGHT examples from Mark's actual testing failures.

Tactical Consistency Guardrails

The V3.4 prompt includes a dedicated "Tactical Consistency Guardrails" section with two subsections: Confidentiality Protection (never include information the user wants private; never reveal walk-away points in suggested communications; mentally check every draft before presenting) and Pricing Consistency (volume discounts go in one direction; verify tier logic; maintain anchor consistency across exchanges). Both use WRONG/RIGHT example pairs from actual coaching failures.
