Executive Summary

The Opportunity

Alley Steakhouse at 1816 N Vermont Ave is showing clear signs of failure. Reviews describe it as "very large and sparsely populated with diners." The food is "good enough" — damning faint praise for an expensive steakhouse. Same hospitality group as Figaro Bistrot (great vibes, mediocre execution). Firsthand visit (Christmas 2025): "Really really bad." This 3,200 sqft space with dramatic bow barrel truss ceilings will likely become available within 12-18 months.

The Strategy: Watch & Prepare

18-month timeline to funding readiness. Use this window to: secure investors, assemble team, refine concept, build relationships with landlord, and position to be first in line when the space becomes available.

The Concept

Good Bones is an all-day community sanctuary where you can spend a whole Sunday — gospel music over biscuits at 9am, transition to wine and small plates at 5pm. Black-owned premium cafe that doesn't announce itself — it just IS.

Section 3: Design Bible

Design Concept Statement

"Cecil B. DeMille estate meets Sunday dinner at Grandma's house. Old Hollywood glamour filtered through soul. The place where Marilyn Monroe would have studied her lines over biscuits and honey butter, where golden hour is always happening, where the brass catches the light just right."

Color Palette

Aged Brass — #C9A962
Primary accent. Hardware, light fixtures, menu boards.
Cream — #FDF8F0
Walls, tablecloths, ceiling. Warm white, not stark.
Espresso — #2C1810
Rich dark brown. Wood tones, leather, text.
Burgundy — #722F37
Accent color. Velvet banquettes, wine references.
Sage — #9CAF88
Secondary accent. Plants, subtle textiles.

Contrast. Iron details, frames, signage accents.

Furniture & Fixtures

Seating

  • Communal Table (1): Reclaimed wood, 12-14 seats, brass hairpin legs. Centerpiece of space.
  • Two-Top Tables (10-12): Marble tops, brass edge trim, bentwood chairs
  • Four-Top Tables (6-8): Same marble/brass, mixed seating (2 chairs + small banquette)
  • Banquettes: Burgundy velvet, tufted, along walls. Deep enough for 3-hour stays.
  • Bar Stools (8-10): Aged leather, brass footrest, swivel
  • Outdoor Patio: French bistro chairs (black metal), marble-top tables

Bar

  • Bar Top: Zinc or brushed brass. Aged patina, not shiny.
  • Back Bar: Dark wood shelving, brass rails, mirror backing
  • Espresso Machine: La Marzocco (visible, brass accents)
  • Coffee Display: Wood crates, glass domes for pastries

Fixtures

  • Main Chandeliers: Art deco-influenced, brass + frosted glass globes. 3-4 over main dining.
  • Wall Sconces: Brass, exposed Edison bulbs, dimmable
  • Pendant Lights: Over bar and communal table, brass cone shades
  • String Lights: Outdoor patio only, warm white, in canopy pattern

Art Direction

Wall Art

  • Gallery Wall (Main): Black-and-white Old Hollywood photographs. Candid shots, not headshots. Dorothy Dandridge, Lena Horne, Sammy Davis Jr., Sidney Poitier. Thin brass frames.
  • Feature Wall: Large-scale botanical print or vintage Los Feliz/Hollywood map. Subtle, not overpowering.
  • Local Artist Partnership: Rotating quarterly exhibition of LA-based Black artists. Small commission on sales.

Textural Elements

  • Plants: Palm fronds (single, dramatic), fiddle leaf figs, pothos trailing from shelves
  • Textiles: Linen napkins (cream), velvet throw pillows on banquettes
  • Wood: Warm walnut tones, visible grain, slightly worn edges

Signature Element (Persephone-Inspired)

The "Wooden Spoon Wall" — A collection of vintage wooden spoons, different sizes and patinas, arranged organically on one wall. References Persephone's famous spoon wall but ties to soul food heritage (grandmother's kitchen).

Space Transition Guide

The Daily Arc: From Sunrise to Sunset

7am: Lights bright. Coffee aroma dominant. Jazz standards low. Papers on communal table. Laptop-friendly energy.

11am: Kitchen comes alive. Bacon, biscuits, savory smells mix with coffee. Music picks up. Conversation volume rises.

3pm: The lull. Lights dim 20%. Music softens. Afternoon tea service. Remote workers settle in. Golden light starts coming through windows.

6pm: THE SHIFT. Lights dim to 50%. Music transitions to evening soul. Candles lit on tables. Wine bottles appear on bar. Menu boards update. Energy shifts from productivity to conviviality.

9pm: Full evening mode. Intimate lighting. Last call for kitchen at 9pm. Wine and small plates. Conversation-driven. Neighborhood gathering spot energy.

Section 6: Risk Assessment

What Could Kill This Concept

HIGH RISK: Execution — First-Time Restaurant Operator

The Reality: Restaurant industry has 60% failure rate in first year, 80% within 5 years. First-time operators fail at even higher rates.

Mitigation:

  • Hire experienced GM (someone who's opened 2+ restaurants)
  • Hire experienced Head Chef (fine dining background + soul food knowledge)
  • Build advisor network: Find 2-3 successful LA restaurant operators willing to advise
  • Consider partnership with experienced operator for equity stake
  • Work in a restaurant for 3-6 months before opening (stage at All Time, Persephone, similar)

MEDIUM RISK: Space Timing

The Reality: Alley Steakhouse is currently operating (struggling, but operating). Space won't be available until they close — predicted 12-18 months. Another tenant could move faster.

Mitigation:

  • Use 18-month runway to: secure funding, build team, refine concept
  • Build relationship with landlord NOW — express long-term interest in space
  • Monitor Alley Steakhouse performance (Yelp reviews, OpenTable availability, foot traffic)
  • Identify 2-3 backup spaces on Vermont Ave (there are 5 retail listings)
  • Get a broker who specializes in restaurant spaces — first to know when it closes

HIGH RISK: Capital Requirements

The Reality: $1.25M is significant. Friends & family round may not get you there. Build-out always goes over budget.

Mitigation:

  • Phase the build-out: Open with simpler evening program, expand later
  • Consider second-generation restaurant space (existing kitchen = lower build-out)
  • Explore SBA loans for portion of capital
  • Build contingency into budget (15% is minimum)
  • Have backup plan if raise stalls at $750K (what's minimum viable?)

MEDIUM RISK: Evening Wine Program Execution

The Reality: All-day cafes often struggle to transition to evening. Customers don't think of it as a dinner spot. Wine program requires different expertise than coffee.

Mitigation:

  • Study Caffe Vita's wine happy hour model (3-5pm transition)
  • Hire someone with wine bar experience, not just restaurant wine list experience
  • Start with limited wine list (20-25 bottles) focused on story/curation
  • Program events: Wine tastings, winemaker dinners (Volunteer Park Cafe model)
  • Physical transformation: Candles, lighting change, visual cues that it's "evening now"

MEDIUM RISK: Gospel Positioning Backlash

The Reality: Gospel music in a place that serves alcohol could alienate religious community. Or could feel appropriative/inauthentic to some.

Mitigation:

  • Be authentic: If you have genuine connection to gospel tradition, lead with that story
  • Sunday alcohol service can be limited or non-existent during gospel hours
  • Partner with local churches for community events
  • Price gospel brunch accessibly (don't make it feel exclusive)
  • Hire staff from the community who understand the culture

MEDIUM RISK: Labor Market

The Reality: 79% of restaurants report being short at least one position. California minimum wage increases. Competition for quality staff is fierce.

Mitigation:

  • Pay above market for key positions (Head Chef, Lead Barista)
  • Create career paths (barista → lead → manager)
  • Benefits: Even basic health insurance is differentiator
  • Culture: Staff who believe in the mission stay longer
  • Cross-train everyone (builds flexibility, reduces single points of failure)

LOW RISK: Competition Response

The Reality: All Time, Maru, or others could add gospel/soul elements.

Why Low Risk: Positioning is hard to copy. All Time's Mediterranean identity can't suddenly become soul. Maru's minimalism can't suddenly become Old Hollywood. The cultural authenticity is the moat.

LOW RISK: ABC License

The Reality: Type 41 (beer/wine) is relatively straightforward to obtain.

Cost: ~$945 application fee

Timeline: ~90 days

Requirement: 51% of revenue from food (should be easy for cafe model)
